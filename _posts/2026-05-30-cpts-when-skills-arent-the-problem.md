I'm 52 years old, 27 years into a career as a sysadmin, and I've been on
Hack The Box regularly since 2024. To keep from getting stuck in the
daily grind at work, I decided to walk the CPTS path. It took two
attempts. In the end I came out with 14 of 14 flags and the certificate.

![Stack of self-help books on a shelf](/assets/img/blog/cpts-when-skills-arent-the-problem/self-help-books.jpg)
*Plenty of wisdom here — the question is where you put your attention.*

You can do what I did: buy a stack of self-help books, line them up on
the shelf, and keep churning the problem over and over in your head.
None of that, on its own, did much for me. What actually helps me is
mindfulness and running.

What doesn't help is keeping the focus on the anxiety itself. Put your
attention on the things you actually enjoy instead. For me — and
probably for you, if you're reading this — that's hacking. :)

There are plenty of blogs about how to prepare for CPTS and how to write
the report. Most are written by people who aren't built like me — I'm
not a hip young confident vlogger. My information needs aren't the same.

Online you hear a lot about how hard the CPTS is, how many people fail
on the report. For someone with a tendency toward self-doubt or
performance anxiety, that drumbeat can be a reason to never even start.
This post is for the reader who recognises that pattern in themselves.
It isn't medical advice — it's how I walked the path.

If your blocker isn't the technical material — if what gets you in
trouble is structure, methodology, and how you behave under stress —
there's a version of this exam you can pass. You miss every shot you
don't take.

## What going off the rails looked like

On my first attempt, my problem wasn't a knowledge gap. It was that I
started moving badly the moment the clock started.

I went in too hot, started enumerating frantically, jumped around when
something didn't yield immediately, and didn't stop to relativise. The
first night I slept poorly. The second night was worse. By day three I
had tunnel vision: I kept digging deeper into the same rabbit hole
instead of stepping back. Sleep deprivation compounded it — the
relativising you can do at full strength stops being available.

The honest moment came when I noticed that this wasn't a skill problem.
The material I had studied was enough. The pattern was behavioural:
panic-driven enumeration, no structure, refusing to sleep, no recovery
from a bad first lead.

If you recognise that pattern, the rest of this post is for you.

## Mindset

A few things to internalise before you click *start*:

1. **You have a second attempt.** Effectively that's 20 days, not 10.
   The clock isn't as tight as it feels.
2. **Submit a report on your first attempt no matter what.** Even if
   you didn't get all the flags. The report itself is graded, and
   submitting one resets your psychology for the retake.
3. **Writing the report takes real time.** A few days, not a few hours.
   Build that into your schedule, not as an afterthought.
4. **Stay close to the course material.** Exotic chains outside what
   HTB Academy covers are unlikely. Trust the syllabus.
5. **Keep breathing.** Calmly. Out loud if you need to. Panic mode is
   not where you do good analysis.

Do the things that give you rest. In panic you can't do a good
analysis. Sleep deprivation does the same. Don't start the box like a
maniac — read what's actually being asked first.

## What helped me

**Practice the report before the exam.** Before sitting CPTS, write a
couple of mock reports in SysReptor based on retired HTB boxes. You'll
get the structure into your muscle memory and discover the time it
takes when there's no pressure on the clock. Read other CPTS students'
blog posts, especially the ones that talk specifically about what the
report needs to contain. That knowledge is portable: you'll use it
during the live engagement to pre-fill sections as you go.

**Document the chain as you build it.** At every step — not just the
successful ones — write down what you tried, what worked, what didn't,
and the relevant CVE or CWE. Take screenshots of the moments that
matter. Don't tell yourself you'll remember; under stress, you won't.
This costs you a few minutes during the live work and saves you hours
when writing the report.

**After the flags, walk it again as a layperson.** Once you have all
the flags, go back through your chain step by step as if you were
explaining it to someone who wasn't there. There should be no gaps.
For each step, ask: what CWE underlies this finding? Writing the
report against the CWE structure makes it more rigorous and helps you
spot weak links in your own reasoning.

## If you're a sysadmin, use it

Don't be shy about bringing your operational background into the
report. If you've spent years actually running the infrastructure
you're now breaking, you have a perspective most pentesters don't:
you know how the defenders will respond to your recommendations,
what's realistic to implement, and what would actually have caught
the chain in production. That makes your remediation advice more
grounded than a textbook recommendation. Put it in the report.

## Closing

You don't have to be a hip young vlogger to do this. You don't have
to be loud, or fast, or confident from the first minute. The exam
asks for technical capability and a coherent report; the rest is
between you and the clock.

If you find that the strategies here aren't enough — if exam pressure
or anxiety is hitting harder than what you can manage on your own —
there's no shame in talking to a professional alongside the practical
work. This post is one person's experience, not a treatment plan.

You miss every shot you don't take.
